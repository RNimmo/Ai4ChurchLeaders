    Protecting vulnerable people in your congregation

    Older adults, recent migrants, and isolated individuals are disproportionately targeted by scammers. As a church community, you are ideally placed to help. Here's what to know — and what to do.

    ⚠ New Zealand context: In 2024, New Zealanders lost over $200 million to scams. Older people (65+) are the most frequently targeted group. Churches are trusted communities — which scammers exploit.
    📞
    Phone and voice scams
    Including "wangiri", bank impersonation, and grandparent scams
    High risk ⌄

    Phone scams are the most common type reported to New Zealand police. AI is now being used to clone family members' voices, making some calls nearly indistinguishable from genuine ones.

    Common patterns targeting older New Zealanders:

    • !"Your grandchild is in trouble" — A caller claims a family member is in trouble and needs money urgently. AI voice cloning can make this caller sound exactly like the person.
    • !Bank impersonation — A caller says they're from ANZ, Westpac, ASB, etc. and your account has been compromised. They ask for your details or to transfer funds to a "safe account".
    • !Tech support fraud — Caller claims to be from Microsoft, Spark, or your internet provider. Says your computer has a virus and they need remote access.
    • !Wangiri ("one-ring" scam) — A missed call from an international number. Calling back connects you to a premium-rate service.
    • !IRD / government impersonation — Caller claims to be from IRD and demands immediate payment to avoid arrest or prosecution.

    What to tell congregation members:

    • ✓Hang up — No bank, government agency, or tech company will ever ask you to transfer money, buy gift cards, or give remote access over the phone. Hanging up is always safe.
    • ✓Call back on a known number — If worried, hang up and call the bank or agency using the number on the back of your card or on their official website.
    • ✓Establish a family safe word — Agree on a code word with family members. If someone calls claiming to be family and can't say the safe word, it's not them.
    • ✓Never feel rushed — Urgency is a scam tactic. Real emergencies allow time to verify. Slow down and check.
    ❤
    Romance and friendship scams
    Long-running deceptions on dating sites, Facebook, and WhatsApp
    High risk ⌄

    Romance scams cause immense emotional and financial harm. Widowed, divorced, and isolated individuals in congregations are frequently targeted. Scammers often spend weeks or months building trust before asking for money.

    • !The offshore professional — An online contact (often claiming to be a widowed doctor, engineer, or military officer) builds a deep connection but can never meet in person. Eventually asks for money for an emergency.
    • !Cryptocurrency investment romance — A new online partner introduces "a great investment opportunity" and encourages the victim to invest through a fake platform. Profits are shown but withdrawal is impossible.
    • !Facebook / WhatsApp impersonation — A fake account pretending to be a friend or acquaintance makes contact and requests money for an emergency.

    How your church community can help:

    • ✓Create a culture where it's normal and safe to ask a trusted friend to review an online relationship without shame.
    • ✓Teach members to do a reverse image search on profile photos (right-click the photo and search Google Images).
    • ✓Gently raise the topic in pastoral conversations with people who seem unusually secretive about a new "online friend".
    • ✓If someone has been scammed, respond with compassion — shame is the scammer's best protection. Help them report it to Netsafe and the bank immediately.
    📈
    Investment and cryptocurrency scams
    Fake platforms, "pig butchering," and impersonated financial advisors
    High risk ⌄

    Investment scams are the highest-value scam type in NZ by total losses. They often begin through social media ads or messaging apps and can target financially astute people, not just the vulnerable.

    • !"Pig butchering" — A long-term relationship is built (often romantic) with the goal of slowly convincing the victim to invest on a fake platform. Funds are "fattened" with fake profits, then the platform disappears.
    • !Celebrity endorsement ads — Social media ads using AI-generated videos of well-known New Zealanders endorsing a crypto platform.
    • !Recovery scams — After being scammed, victims are contacted by "recovery agents" who claim they can retrieve the lost money for an upfront fee.
    • ✓Check any investment platform with the FMA register at fma.govt.nz before investing.
    • ✓If you can only profit by recruiting others, it's likely a pyramid scheme — walk away.
    • ✓No legitimate recovery service exists for crypto losses. Anyone claiming otherwise is a second scam.
    🎁
    Charity and donation scams
    Fake charities, door-to-door collectors, and emergency appeals
    Medium risk ⌄

    Charitable and generous people in faith communities are specifically targeted by fake charity scams, particularly after natural disasters or during the Christmas season.

    • !Fake charity names that closely resemble well-known organisations (e.g. "NZ Red Crescent" instead of the Red Cross).
    • !Unsolicited door-to-door or phone collectors asking for cash only.
    • !Urgent disaster-relief emails or social media posts linking to unofficial donation pages.
    • ✓Verify any charity at charities.govt.nz before donating.
    • ✓Donate directly through an organisation's official website — not through links in emails or social posts.
    • ✓Legitimate collectors always carry ID and will never pressure you for immediate cash.
    🤖
    AI voice cloning and impersonation
    Calls that sound like family, clergy, or trusted contacts
    Emerging high risk ⌄

    This is a rapidly growing threat that many people are not aware of yet. AI tools can now generate a convincing voice clone from a few seconds of audio — available from Facebook videos, YouTube, or even a voicemail.

    • !Family emergency calls — A call that sounds exactly like a grandchild, child, or spouse says they're in trouble and need money immediately.
    • !Clergy impersonation — A caller claiming to be (and sounding like) the vicar, pastor, or bishop asking for a financial favour.
    • !WhatsApp voice message cloning — A voice message in a group chat sounding like a trusted member making an urgent request.
    💡 Teach your congregation this rule: A convincing voice is no longer proof of identity. For any unexpected financial request — no matter how convincing the caller sounds — hang up and call back on a number you already have.
    • ✓Set up a family "safe word" that must be said for any financial request — something not publicly known.
    • ✓If a caller says "don't tell anyone" — that is the scam. Always tell someone.
    • ✓Raise awareness in your congregation by talking about this threat directly — from the pulpit if appropriate.

    How your church community can be a line of defence

    • ✓Make it easy to ask — Regularly remind members that they can always run something past a trusted person in the church before acting. No shame, no judgment.
    • ✓Run regular awareness conversations — A 5-minute mention in a Sunday bulletin or small group can plant seeds that save someone thousands of dollars.
    • ✓Connect isolated members — Loneliness increases vulnerability. Regular pastoral contact reduces the conditions that scammers exploit.
    • ✓Have a clear protocol — Make it known: "If anyone in our congregation is approached by someone suspicious, call [name] on [number]."
    • ✓Respond to scam victims with compassion — Many victims never report because of shame. The church should be a safe place to disclose without judgment.

    Report a scam & find help

    If you or someone in your congregation has been targeted or affected by a scam, here's where to go. Reporting helps protect others.

    ⚠ If money has been lost: Contact your bank immediately — they may be able to stop or reverse a transfer. Then report to Netsafe and Police.

    Report scams in New Zealand

    🛡
    Netsafe — Report online harm
    NZ's primary online safety organisation. Report scams, phishing, and online abuse.
    netsafe.org.nz/report
    👨‍💪
    NZ Police — Report fraud
    Report significant financial fraud. For emergencies call 111; non-urgent: 105.
    police.govt.nz
    🔐
    CERT NZ — Cyber incidents
    Report phishing emails, account compromises, and malware to NZ's cyber security agency.
    cert.govt.nz
    📈
    FMA — Investment scams
    Report fake investment schemes and check if a financial service is registered.
    fma.govt.nz
    🛒
    Consumer Protection — Scams
    Report and learn about consumer scams including fake charities and online shopping fraud.
    consumerprotection.govt.nz
    💌
    DIA — Report spam
    Report unsolicited commercial email (spam) to the Department of Internal Affairs.
    dia.govt.nz/spam
