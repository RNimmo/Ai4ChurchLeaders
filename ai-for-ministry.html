AI for Ministry

Practical prompts and guidance to help church leaders use AI wisely — saving time on draining tasks so you can be more present for the people and work that matters.

AI is a brilliant first draft, never a final authority.
The big idea

AI is a very capable writing and thinking assistant — not magic, and not a replacement for your pastoral heart. It doesn't know your congregation, your theology, or your relationships. You bring all of that. Use it to handle draining tasks so you have more energy for people and prayer.

Choose any tab above to explore ready-to-use prompts for common ministry tasks. Each prompt can be copied and pasted straight into your AI tool of choice — then personalise from there. The copy buttons make it easy on your phone.

The main AI tools

ChatGPT

Made by OpenAI. Free to start. The most widely known AI tool. Excellent for writing, drafting, planning, and research. The free version (GPT-4o) is very capable for most ministry tasks.

AI works best as a thinking partner for sermon prep — helping you explore angles, find illustrations, or write discussion questions. Your exegesis, your pastoral voice, and your prayerful discernment remain entirely yours.

Fresh sermon angles

When you’re stuck on how to approach a passage

I'm preaching on [passage, e.g. John 6:1-15] this Sunday. My congregation includes [describe your people briefly, e.g. young families, long-term Christians, people new to faith]. Give me three fresh angles on this passage I might not have considered, and suggest one contemporary illustration for each angle.
Any AI tool

Small group discussion questions

Turn a sermon into a week of community conversation

Based on a sermon on [passage or theme], write 5 discussion questions for a small group. Make them progressively deeper - starting with observation, then reflection, then personal application. Include one question that might challenge longer-term Christians and one that's accessible to someone newer to faith.
Any AI tool

Sermon series outline

Plan several weeks in one go

Help me plan a 4-week sermon series on [theme or book of the Bible]. Suggest a title for the series, a title for each week, a key passage for each week, and one central question the sermon should answer. Keep it grounded in Scripture and accessible to a mixed congregation.
Any AI tool

Children’s talk version

Adapt a passage for younger ears

I'm preaching on [passage] for adults. Help me write a short 3-minute children's talk on the same theme for ages 5-10. Use a simple object lesson or story, avoid jargon, and end with one clear takeaway that a child could repeat back.
Any AI tool

Contextual background research

Historical and cultural context quickly summarised

Give me a brief summary of the historical and cultural context of [passage, e.g. the Sermon on the Mount / Nehemiah rebuilding the walls]. What would the original audience have understood that a modern reader might miss? Keep it accessible - I want to weave this into a sermon, not write an academic paper.
Any AI tool
Heads up

AI can be confidently wrong on theology — especially on nuanced or denominational matters. Always bring your own discernment and cross-check anything doctrinal before using it. AI is a starting point, not a commentary.

Save hours on document drafting

Rewrite for clarity

Simplify something that’s become too long or formal

Please rewrite the following text so it is clearer, warmer, and easier to read. Aim for plain language at about a Year 10 reading level. Keep all the key information but cut anything repetitive. Here is the original: [paste your text]

Annual report section

Turn activity notes into a polished report narrative

Here are rough notes about what our church did this year in [area, e.g. community outreach / youth ministry / pastoral care]: [paste notes]. Please write a 200-word section for our annual report. Keep it warm, honest, and grateful in tone - highlighting people and impact rather than just activity counts.

AI can help you prepare thoughtful responses and resources for pastoral situations — but remember, it doesn’t know the person. Use it to find words, never to replace presence.

Important: protect privacy always

Never include real names, identifying details, or confidential disclosures in an AI prompt. Use generic descriptions like “a church member” or “someone in my congregation” instead.

Finding words for a difficult conversation

When you need to start a hard meeting or email

I need to have a difficult pastoral conversation with a church member about [describe the general situation without naming anyone, e.g. "someone who has been causing conflict in a team"]. Help me think through how to approach this with care and clarity. What might I say to open the conversation? What should I be careful about?
Works well in Claude

Sympathy or condolence message

When words don’t come easily

Help me write a short, heartfelt message to someone in my congregation who has recently lost [e.g. a spouse / a parent / a child]. Keep it warm and grounded in Christian hope without being platitudinous. About 3-4 sentences. I'll personalise it before sending.
Any AI tool

Prayer or liturgy for a specific situation

A starting point for a service, visit, or card

Write a short prayer (about 100 words) for [describe situation, e.g. a family facing serious illness / a couple preparing for marriage / a community grieving a sudden loss]. Use accessible, non-jargon language that someone unfamiliar with church would still find meaningful.
Any AI tool

Prepare for a pastoral visit

Conversation starter ideas for specific situations

I'm visiting a church member who is [describe situation generally, e.g. recently bereaved / living with a long-term illness / going through a marriage breakdown]. Suggest 3-4 open questions I could use to open conversation well. Also, are there any topics or phrases I should be careful to avoid in this situation?
Works well in Claude

Planning events and communicating them across multiple channels takes time. AI can help you turn one idea into a full suite of communications quickly.

One idea, many formats

Turn one event or sermon into a week of content

Here is a short summary of our upcoming [event/sermon/announcement]: [paste summary]. From this, please write: (1) a short Facebook post, (2) a 3-sentence email newsletter intro, (3) a bulletin notice under 50 words, and (4) a short text message version for SMS or WhatsApp. Keep the tone warm and inviting.

Event planning checklist

Don’t miss anything — especially for new events

I'm planning [describe the event, e.g. a community Christmas carols evening for around 200 people at a church in New Zealand]. Create a practical planning checklist with tasks grouped by timing - 6 weeks out, 3 weeks out, 1 week out, and day of event. Include things that churches often forget.

Volunteer recruitment message

Inviting people to serve in an engaging way

Include what the role involves, why it matters, and how people can express interest. About 150 words. Avoid guilt-based language.

How to write a good image prompt

The formula

[Subject] + [Setting or mood] + [Style] + [What to avoid]

Newsletter header image

A welcoming image for the top of a church newsletter

A warm, inviting photograph of a small community gathering around a table in a light-filled room, soft natural light, diverse group of people, joyful and peaceful atmosphere, photorealistic style. No text in the image.

AI image cautions

AI image tools sometimes get hands wrong, add unreadable text, or create unintentionally odd details. Always check before publishing. Avoid asking AI to depict Jesus or biblical figures specifically — the results are often theologically and culturally problematic.

Using AI wisely means knowing where it helps and where to be careful. Here’s a simple framework for church leaders.

Generally fine — go ahead
Drafting, editing, and summarising text you will review before using
Research and brainstorming — exploring ideas, illustrations, angles
Admin writing — emails, notices, meeting summaries, reports
Planning tools — event checklists, series outlines, communication calendars
Royalty-free image creation for newsletters, slides, and social media
Learning — asking AI to explain a concept or summarise a topic
Use with care — your judgement required
Theological questions — AI can be confidently wrong; always cross-check important claims
Pastoral language — AI doesn’t know the person; always personalise before sending
Anything going out under your name — read it carefully, make it genuinely yours
AI images of people — check carefully for errors (hands, faces, text in images)
Sensitive topics — AI may give generic responses to things that need nuance and care
Don’t do this
Sharing confidential pastoral information — never include real names or sensitive situations
Using AI output without reading it — it can be wrong, and it will go out under your name
Copying AI content into official legal or safeguarding documents without expert review
Asking AI to depict Jesus or biblical figures — results are often theologically problematic
Treating AI as a replacement for prayer, discernment, or genuine human presence

A note on privacy

Free AI tools like ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini may use your conversations to improve their models. Avoid entering anything you would not be comfortable being seen by others — especially names, personal situations, or confidential disclosures. For sensitive work, check your organisation’s data policy, and consider whether a paid or enterprise plan with stronger privacy protections is appropriate.

Copyright & AI images

Images generated by AI tools are generally free for you to use — but check the terms of the specific tool. Adobe Firefly is the safest option as it is trained only on licensed content. Avoid using AI to recreate specific artists’ styles or existing artworks.
