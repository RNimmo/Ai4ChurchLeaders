AI for Ministry

Practical prompts and guidance to help church leaders use AI wisely — saving time on draining tasks so you can be more present for the people and work that matters.

AI is a brilliant first draft, never a final authority.
The big idea

AI is a very capable writing and thinking assistant — not a replacement for your pastoral heart. It doesn’t know your congregation, your theology, or your relationships. You bring all of that. Use it to handle draining tasks so you have more energy for people and prayer.

AI works best as a thinking partner for sermon prep — helping you explore angles, find illustrations, or write discussion questions. Your exegesis, your pastoral voice, and your prayerful discernment remain entirely yours.

Fresh sermon angles

When you’re stuck on how to approach a passage

I'm preaching on [passage, e.g. John 6:1-15] this Sunday. My congregation includes [describe your people briefly, e.g. young families, long-term Christians, people new to faith]. Give me three fresh angles on this passage I might not have considered, and suggest one contemporary illustration for each angle.

Small group discussion questions

Turn a sermon into a week of community conversation

Based on a sermon on [passage or theme], write 5 discussion questions for a small group. Make them progressively deeper - starting with observation, then reflection, then personal application. Include one question that might challenge longer-term Christians and one that's accessible to someone newer to faith.
Any AI tool

Sermon series outline

Plan several weeks in one go

Help me plan a 4-week sermon series on [theme or book of the Bible]. Suggest a title for the series, a title for each week, a key passage for each week, and one central question the sermon should answer. Keep it grounded in Scripture and accessible to a mixed congregation.
Any AI tool

Children’s talk version

Adapt a passage for younger ears

I'm preaching on [passage] for adults. Help me write a short 3-minute children's talk on the same theme for ages 5-10. Use a simple object lesson or story, avoid jargon, and end with one clear takeaway that a child could repeat back.
Any AI tool

Historical and cultural context

Background that helps a passage come alive

Give me a brief summary of the historical and cultural context of [passage, e.g. the Sermon on the Mount / Nehemiah rebuilding the walls]. What would the original audience have understood that a modern reader might miss? Keep it accessible - I want to weave this into a sermon, not write an academic paper.
Any AI tool
Heads up

AI can be confidently wrong on theology — especially on nuanced or denominational matters. Always bring your own discernment and cross-check anything doctrinal. AI is a starting point, not a commentary.

AI has a limited and specific role in pastoral care. It can help you prepare — finding words for a card, drafting a prayer, or thinking through a difficult conversation in advance. It is not a substitute for human presence, professional supervision, or the advice of a trusted colleague.

For actual pastoral situations — talk to a person

For real pastoral challenges — a difficult conversation, a safeguarding concern, a complex family situation, a person in crisis — your supervisor, a trusted colleague, or a professional counsellor is always the right first call. AI does not know the person, cannot exercise pastoral judgement, and should never replace professional supervision.

Always protect privacy

Never include real names, identifying details, or confidential disclosures in any AI prompt. Use generic descriptions only — “a church member” or “someone in my congregation”.

Where AI can help: preparation and written communication

Condolence or sympathy message

When words don’t come easily — a starting point only

Help me write a short, heartfelt message to someone in my congregation who has recently lost [e.g. a spouse / a parent / a child]. Keep it warm and grounded in Christian hope without being platitudinous. About 3-4 sentences. I'll personalise it before sending.
Any AI tool

Prayer for a specific situation

A starting point for a service, visit, or card

Write a short prayer (about 100 words) for [describe situation generally, e.g. a family facing serious illness / a couple preparing for marriage / a community grieving a sudden loss]. Use accessible, non-jargon language that someone unfamiliar with church would still find meaningful.
Any AI tool

Prepare open questions for a visit

Conversation starter ideas for specific situations

I'm visiting a church member who is [describe situation generally, e.g. recently bereaved / living with a long-term illness]. Suggest 3-4 open questions I could use to open conversation well. Also, are there any topics or phrases I should be careful to avoid in this situation?

Prompts for general AI tools

Explain a passage in plain language

Useful for teaching prep or personal study

Please explain [passage, e.g. Romans 8:1-17] in plain, accessible language. What is the main point? What would a first-time reader struggle to understand? What are 2-3 key ideas that someone could take away from this passage?
Any AI tool

Explore a theological question

Understand how different traditions approach an issue

I'd like to understand how different Christian traditions approach the question of [theological topic, e.g. the nature of baptism / spiritual gifts / salvation and free will]. Please summarise the main positions briefly and fairly, noting where Anglican thinking typically sits. I'm not looking for a definitive answer, just a map of the landscape.
Works well in Claude
Important caution

General AI tools like ChatGPT and Claude can be confidently wrong on theology — sometimes subtly so. The YouVersion CEO has noted AI Bible misquote rates of 15–60% depending on the tool. Specialist tools like Logos are safer for doctrinal questions because they draw from verified theological sources. Always cross-check anything significant.

AI translation has become genuinely useful for communicating across language barriers — making newsletters, notices, and simple resources accessible to congregation members whose first language isn’t English. It is not perfect, and for important documents a fluent speaker should always check the output.

Best practice

AI translation is strongest for widely spoken languages (Spanish, Mandarin, Samoan, Tongan, Hindi, Tagalog) and weaker for languages with less online content. For anything going on signage or in a printed bulletin, always ask a fluent speaker to review it before publishing. For te reo Māori, consider using a specialist service or a kaumātua review.

Translate a newsletter

Make your regular communication accessible to all

Please translate the following church newsletter into [language, e.g. Samoan / Tongan / Mandarin / Hindi]. Keep the tone warm and friendly. If any phrase doesn't translate well culturally, please note it and suggest an alternative. Here is the newsletter: [paste text]
Any AI tool

Always get a human check

AI translation can be surprisingly good, but it makes mistakes — especially with Pacific languages, te reo Māori, and less commonly spoken languages. Before anything is printed or displayed, ask a fluent speaker from your congregation or community to review it. This also builds relationship and shows genuine respect.

How to write a good image prompt

The formula

[Subject] + [Setting or mood] + [Style] + [What to avoid]

Newsletter header image

A welcoming image for the top of a church newsletter

A warm, inviting photograph of a small community gathering around a table in a light-filled room, soft natural light, diverse group of people, joyful and peaceful atmosphere, photorealistic style. No text in the image.

Minimalist flat illustration of [describe event theme, e.g. candles and stars for a Christmas service / open hands and sunlight for Easter], warm colour palette, square format suitable for Instagram or Facebook, space at the top for overlaid text. Clean and modern style.

AI image cautions

Always check AI images carefully before publishing — hands, faces, and text within images can go wrong. Avoid asking AI to depict Jesus or biblical figures; the results are often theologically and culturally problematic. Adobe Firefly is the safest option as it is trained only on licensed content.

Using AI wisely means knowing where it helps and where to be careful. Here’s a simple framework for church leaders.

Generally fine — go ahead
Drafting, editing, and summarising text you will review before using
Research and brainstorming — exploring ideas, illustrations, angles
Admin writing — emails, notices, meeting summaries, reports
Planning tools — event checklists, series outlines, communication calendars
Royalty-free image creation for newsletters, slides, and social media
Translation of newsletters, notices, and signage (with a human review)
Bible study support — context, questions, reflection prompts
Use with care — your judgement required
Theological questions — AI can be confidently wrong; always cross-check important claims
Pastoral preparation — AI can help you find words, but not replace wisdom or presence
Anything going out under your name — read it carefully, make it genuinely yours
AI images of people — check carefully for errors before publishing
Translated content — always ask a fluent speaker to review before printing or displaying
Don’t do this
Sharing confidential pastoral information — never include real names or sensitive situations
Using AI as a substitute for pastoral supervision, a colleague, or a professional counsellor
Using AI output without reading it — it can be wrong, and it goes out under your name
Copying AI into official legal or safeguarding documents without expert review
Asking AI to depict Jesus or biblical figures — results are often theologically problematic

A note on privacy

Free AI tools like ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini may use your conversations to improve their models. Avoid entering anything you would not be comfortable being seen by others — especially names, personal situations, or confidential disclosures. For sensitive work, check your organisation’s data policy.

Copyright & AI images

Images generated by AI tools are generally free for you to use — but check the terms of the specific tool. Adobe Firefly is the safest option as it is trained only on licensed content. Avoid using AI to recreate specific artists’ styles or existing artworks.
